Colonial Nigeria

By the late 19th century, Britain had extended its control across much of West Africa. Nigeria officially became a British colony in 1914 when the Northern and Southern Protectorates were amalgamated. The system of indirect rule, popularized by Lord Lugard, allowed traditional rulers to govern locally under British supervision.

While this reduced the cost of administration, it sowed divisions by favoring some regions over others. Infrastructure like railways and roads were built primarily to extract natural resources such as palm oil and cocoa, enriching Britain while impoverishing Nigerians.

Yet, education introduced by Christian missionaries gave rise to a new class of Nigerians ‚Äî literate, ambitious, and eager for self-rule. By the 1940s, nationalist movements gained strength under leaders like Nnamdi Azikiwe, Obafemi Awolowo, and Ahmadu Bello, uniting Nigerians toward independence.
